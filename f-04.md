## Day 7: The Transfer Function - The System's Soul (S&S/ECA)

### 1.1 Why Laplace? Fourier's Fatal Flaw

In File 3, we used Fourier Series to break periodic signals into sine waves. But Fourier has a fatal flaw: **It requires the signal to be absolutely integrable.**
If a signal grows to infinity (like $e^{2t}$, an unstable system), the Fourier integral blows up. Fourier throws its hands in the air and says "I can't analyze this."

Enter Pierre-Simon Laplace. He asked: "What if I multiply the signal by a decaying exponential $e^{-\sigma t}$ before I integrate it?"
$$\mathcal{L}\{x(t)\} = X(s) = \int_{0}^{\infty} x(t) e^{-st} dt$$
Where $s = \sigma + j\omega$ is a *complex* frequency.

If $x(t) = e^{2t}$ (growing), and we choose $\sigma = 3$, then $e^{2t} \cdot e^{-3t} = e^{-t}$ (decaying). The integral converges! **The Laplace transform can analyze unstable systems.** It is Fourier on steroids.

### 1.2 The s-Domain: Impedance on Steroids

In File 1, we learned that phasors turned derivatives into $j\omega$.
Laplace turns derivatives into $s$, and integrals into $1/s$.

*   $\frac{dv}{dt} \implies sV(s) - v(0^-)$
*   $\int v dt \implies \frac{V(s)}{s} + \frac{i(0^-)}{s}$

Notice that Laplace **automatically includes initial conditions** ($v(0^-)$ and $i(0^-)$). Phasors ignored initial conditions (assuming steady-state). Laplace accounts for them, meaning it can solve transient problems *without* needing the $x(\infty) + [x(0)-x(\infty)]e^{-t/\tau}$ template from File 2.

**The s-Domain Impedances (Assuming zero initial conditions for simplicity):**
*   Resistor: $Z_R = R$
*   Inductor: $Z_L = sL$
*   Capacitor: $Z_C = \frac{1}{sC}$

> **Engineer's Cheat Code:**
> To convert a circuit to the s-domain: replace sources with $V(s)$, replace $R, L, C$ with $R, sL, 1/sC$, and then solve it exactly like a DC circuit using KVL/KCL/Thevenin. The algebra will yield a rational function of $s$.

### 1.3 Defining $H(s)$: Poles, Zeros, and the Eigenvalue Connection

The **Transfer Function** $H(s)$ is the ratio of the output Laplace transform to the input Laplace transform, assuming all initial conditions are zero.
$$H(s) = \frac{Y(s)}{X(s)} = \frac{N(s)}{D(s)} = K \frac{(s - z_1)(s - z_2)...}{(s - p_1)(s - p_2)...}$$

*   **Zeros ($z_i$):** The roots of the numerator $N(s)$. When $s = z_i$, $H(s) = 0$. The system completely blocks that frequency.
*   **Poles ($p_i$):** The roots of the denominator $D(s)$. When $s = p_i$, $H(s) \to \infty$. The system resonates wildly at that frequency.

> **Physical Reality Callout: Poles are Eigenvalues**
> In File 0, we learned that the roots of the characteristic equation of an ODE are eigenvalues. Look at the denominator $D(s)$. It *is* the characteristic equation of the system!
> If a pole is on the left half of the s-plane ($\sigma < 0$, e.g., $s = -2 \pm j3$), the time-domain response contains $e^{-2t}$, which decays. **Stable.**
> If a pole is on the right half ($\sigma > 0$, e.g., $s = +2 \pm j3$), it contains $e^{+2t}$, which grows to infinity. **Unstable (Oscillator/Explosion).**
> If a pole is exactly on the $j\omega$ axis ($\sigma = 0$, e.g., $s = \pm j10$), it is a pure sine wave that lasts forever. **Marginally Stable.**

### 1.4 The "Delicacy" Problem: Deriving $H(s)$ for an RLC Filter

**The Setup:**
Find the transfer function $H(s) = \frac{V_{out}(s)}{V_{in}(s)}$ for a series RLC circuit where the output is taken across the capacitor.

```text
      Vin(s)          sL           1/sC
o---[Z_R]---+---[Z_L]---+---||---o--- Vout(s)
  R         |            |        |
           |           GND      GND
           |
          GND
```

**The Grind:**
This is a voltage divider in the s-domain!
$$V_{out}(s) = V_{in}(s) \times \frac{Z_C}{Z_R + Z_L + Z_C}$$
$$H(s) = \frac{\frac{1}{sC}}{R + sL + \frac{1}{sC}}$$

To put this in standard form, multiply the top and bottom by $sC$:
$$H(s) = \frac{1}{s^2LC + sRC + 1}$$

Let's divide by $LC$ to make the $s^2$ coefficient 1:
$$H(s) = \frac{1/LC}{s^2 + s\frac{R}{L} + \frac{1}{LC}}$$

**Connecting to File 0 and File 3:**
Look at the denominator: $s^2 + s\frac{R}{L} + \frac{1}{LC}$.
Compare this to the standard form from File 0: $s^2 + 2\alpha s + \omega_0^2$.
*   $\omega_0^2 = \frac{1}{LC} \implies \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$ (Resonant frequency!)
*   $2\alpha = \frac{R}{L} \implies \alpha = \frac{R}{2L}$ (Damping factor!)

**The Joy of Understanding:**
Without solving a single differential equation, just by doing simple s-domain algebra, we extracted the exact resonant frequency and damping characteristics of the circuit. The Transfer Function $H(s)$ is the complete DNA of the system. If you have $H(s)$, you own the circuit.

## Day 8: The BJT - Taming the Non-Linear Beast (ADE)

### 2.1 The Physics of the BJT: The Current-Controlled Valve

A BJT is a 3-terminal device: Base (B), Collector (C), Emitter (E).
Think of it as a water valve. The Base is the handle. The Collector is the high-pressure water supply. The Emitter is the drain.

A tiny current ($I_B$) flowing into the base opens the valve, allowing a massive current ($I_C$) to flow from collector to emitter.
The relationship is: $I_C = \beta I_B$, where $\beta$ (beta) is the current gain, typically between 50 and 300.

**The Exponential Truth (Ebers-Moll):**
In reality, the BJT is controlled by the Base-Emitter voltage, not current.
$I_C = I_S e^{V_{BE}/V_T}$
This is the same diode equation from File 3! A BJT is basically two back-to-back diodes, but constructed so that one diode's current is amplified by the physics of the narrow base region.

### 2.2 The Q-Point: Why We Need DC Bias

Look at the equation $I_C = I_S e^{V_{BE}/V_T}$. It is massively non-linear. If you put an AC audio signal directly on the base, the positive half-cycle might produce $10\text{mA}$, but the negative half-cycle might produce $0\text{mA}$ (because the diode cuts off below $0.7\text{V}$). Your output is a distorted mess.

**The Solution:** We must add a DC offset (Bias) to "lift" the AC signal entirely into the linear amplifying region.
The **Quiescent Point (Q-Point)** is the specific DC voltage and current ($I_{C,Q}$, $V_{CE,Q}$) the transistor sits at when no AC signal is applied.

> **Physical Reality Callout: The Active Region**
> To act as a linear amplifier, the BJT MUST be biased in the "Active Region".
> 1. Base-Emitter junction is Forward Biased ($V_{BE} \approx 0.7\text{V}$).
> 2. Base-Collector junction is Reverse Biased ($V_{CE} > 0.3\text{V}$).
     > If $V_{CE}$ drops below $0.3\text{V}$, the transistor enters "Saturation" (the valve is fully open, $I_C$ maxes out, acts like a short circuit). If $I_B = 0$, it's in "Cutoff" (valve closed, acts like an open circuit).

### 2.3 The "Delicacy" Problem: Voltage Divider Bias via Thevenin

How do we establish the Q-Point reliably? The best circuit is the Voltage Divider Bias. It uses two resistors to set the base voltage, making the Q-point nearly independent of the transistor's messy $\beta$ variations.

**The Setup:**
Find the Q-point ($I_C, V_{CE}$) for the following circuit.
$V_{CC} = 12\text{V}$. $R_1 = 47\text{k}\Omega$, $R_2 = 10\text{k}\Omega$. $R_C = 2\text{k}\Omega$, $R_E = 1\text{k}\Omega$. Assume $\beta = 100$. Silicon BJT.

```text
           VCC (+12V)
             |
            [R1] 47k
             |
             +-----------> To Base (B)
             |          
            [R2] 10k     C
             |           |
             |          [RC] 2k
             |           |
            GND          +-----------> To Collector (C)
                         |
                        [RE] 1k
                         |
                        GND           E (Emitter directly to RE)
```

**The Grind (The Industry Way):**
**Step 1: Thevenize the Base Circuit.**
Look back from the base into the $R_1, R_2$ divider. Replace it with $V_{th}$ and $R_{th}$.
*   $V_{th} = V_{CC} \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} = 12 \times \frac{10}{47 + 10} = 12 \times \frac{10}{57} \approx 2.1\text{V}$.
*   $R_{th} = R_1 \parallel R_2 = \frac{47 \times 10}{47 + 10} = \frac{470}{57} \approx 8.25\text{k}\Omega$.

**Step 2: Write the Base-Emitter KVL Loop.**
Start at $V_{th}$, go down through $R_{th}$, drop across the Base-Emitter junction ($0.7\text{V}$), and drop across $R_E$.
$$V_{th} = I_B R_{th} + V_{BE} + I_E R_E$$

**Step 3: The Approximation that Saves Careers.**
We know $I_E = I_B + I_C = I_B + \beta I_B = (\beta + 1)I_B$.
Substitute into KVL: $V_{th} = I_B R_{th} + 0.7 + (\beta + 1)I_B R_E$.
Solve for $I_B$: $I_B = \frac{V_{th} - 0.7}{R_{th} + (\beta + 1)R_E}$.

Look at the denominator. $R_{th} \approx 8.25\text{k}\Omega$. $(\beta + 1)R_E = 101 \times 1\text{k}\Omega = 101\text{k}\Omega$.
Because $(\beta + 1)R_E \gg R_{th}$, the $R_{th}$ term is negligible.
**The Golden Rule of Biasing:** If $(\beta + 1)R_E \ge 10 \times R_{th}$, you can ignore $R_{th}$ entirely!

*Approximate Equation:* $V_{th} \approx 0.7 + I_E R_E$
$$I_E \approx \frac{V_{th} - 0.7}{R_E} = \frac{2.1 - 0.7}{1\text{k}\Omega} = 1.4\text{mA}$$

Since $\beta$ is large, $I_C \approx I_E = 1.4\text{mA}$. (Look! We found $I_C$ without even using $\beta$. This circuit is immune to $\beta$ changes!)

**Step 4: Find $V_{CE}$.**
KVL from Collector to Emitter:
$$V_{CC} = I_C R_C + V_{CE} + I_E R_E$$
$$12 = (1.4\text{mA})(2\text{k}\Omega) + V_{CE} + (1.4\text{mA})(1\text{k}\Omega)$$
$$12 = 2.8\text{V} + V_{CE} + 1.4\text{V}$$
$$V_{CE} = 12 - 4.2 = 7.8\text{V}$$

**The Q-Point is $(1.4\text{mA}, 7.8\text{V})$.** Since $V_{CE} = 7.8\text{V} > 0.3\text{V}$, the transistor is perfectly in the active region. We are ready to amplify.

### 2.4 The Small-Signal Model: The Ultimate Engineering Hack

We have a DC Q-point. Now we apply a tiny AC signal (e.g., $v_{in} = 10\text{mV}$ peak) to the base.

The total base voltage is $V_{BE}(t) = V_{BE,Q} + v_{be}(t)$.
The total collector current is $I_C(t) = I_S e^{(V_{BE,Q} + v_{be})/V_T} = \left( I_S e^{V_{BE,Q}/V_T} \right) \cdot e^{v_{be}/V_T}$.
Notice the first bracket is just $I_{C,Q}$.
$I_C(t) = I_{C,Q} \cdot e^{v_{be}/V_T}$.

If $v_{be}$ is *small* (much less than $V_T \approx 26\text{mV}$), we can use the Taylor series expansion for $e^x \approx 1 + x$.
$I_C(t) \approx I_{C,Q} \left( 1 + \frac{v_{be}}{V_T} \right) = I_{C,Q} + \frac{I_{C,Q}}{V_T} v_{be}$.

The AC component of the current is:
$$i_c = \underbrace{\left( \frac{I_{C,Q}}{V_T} \right)}_{g_m} v_{be}$$

**Boom. Linearity achieved.**
We define **Transconductance ($g_m$)** as the slope of the IC vs VBE curve at the Q-point. (We derived this in File 0!).
$$g_m = \frac{I_{C,Q}}{V_T} = \frac{1.4\text{mA}}{26\text{mV}} \approx 53.8 \text{ mA/V}$$

**The Hybrid-Pi Model:**
To analyze the AC circuit, we turn off all DC sources (short $V_{CC}$ to ground, open current sources). We replace the BJT with the Hybrid-Pi model:
1.  A resistor $r_\pi = \frac{\beta V_T}{I_{C,Q}}$ between Base and Emitter.
2.  A current source $g_m v_{be}$ pointing from Collector to Emitter.

```text
AC Equivalent Circuit (Hybrid-Pi):

       B o---[r_pi]---o--- E
                   |
                 ( g_m * v_be )  (Current source points UP from E to C)
                   |
       C o----------+ 
```
*(Note: $v_{be}$ is the voltage ACROSS $r_\pi$).*

### 2.5 The "Delicacy" Problem: Calculating Voltage Gain ($A_v$)

Let's find the voltage gain $A_v = \frac{v_{out}}{v_{in}}$ for the biased circuit from section 2.3, assuming a load resistor $R_L = 5\text{k}\Omega$ is attached to the collector via a coupling capacitor.

**The Grind:**
**Step 1: Draw the AC Equivalent.**
*   Short $V_{CC}$ to GND. This puts $R_C$ in parallel with $R_L$. Let $R_C' = R_C \parallel R_L = 2\text{k}\Omega \parallel 5\text{k}\Omega \approx 1.43\text{k}\Omega$.
*   Because of the coupling capacitor, $R_1, R_2$ do not affect the AC signal (the capacitor blocks them). The AC signal sees a path straight to the base.

**Step 2: Apply Thevenin to the Input (The Pro Move).**
Students often mess up gain by ignoring the resistance of the source or the transistor's base. Let's be rigorous.
The input voltage $v_{in}$ is applied across the series combination of the base resistance $r_\pi$ and the emitter resistance $R_E$.
Wait, is $R_E$ in the AC loop?
Yes! In this configuration (Emitter unbypassed), the AC current $i_e \approx i_c$ flows down through $R_E$ to ground.
By KVL from Base to Ground:
$$v_{in} = i_b r_\pi + i_e R_E$$
Since $i_e = (\beta + 1)i_b \approx \beta i_b$:
$$v_{in} = i_b r_\pi + \beta i_b R_E = i_b (r_\pi + \beta R_E)$$

What is $v_{be}$? It is just the voltage across $r_\pi$: $v_{be} = i_b r_\pi$.
So, $\frac{v_{be}}{v_{in}} = \frac{i_b r_\pi}{i_b (r_\pi + \beta R_E)} = \frac{r_\pi}{r_\pi + \beta R_E}$.

Let's calculate the numbers:
$r_\pi = \frac{\beta V_T}{I_C} = \frac{100 \times 26\text{mV}}{1.4\text{mA}} \approx 1.85\text{k}\Omega$.
$\beta R_E = 100 \times 1\text{k}\Omega = 100\text{k}\Omega$.
Notice $100\text{k}\Omega \gg 1.85\text{k}\Omega$. Therefore, $v_{be} \approx \frac{r_\pi}{\beta R_E} v_{in}$.

**Step 3: Calculate Output Voltage.**
At the collector, the current $g_m v_{be}$ flows through the parallel collector resistance $R_C'$.
$$v_{out} = - (g_m v_{be}) R_C'$$
*(Negative sign because current flows UP from ground through the current source to the positive $V_{CC}$ rail, but we measure $v_{out}$ from collector to ground. The current pulls the collector voltage DOWN).*

**Step 4: Compute Gain.**
$$A_v = \frac{v_{out}}{v_{in}} = \frac{-g_m v_{be} R_C'}{v_{in}} = -g_m R_C' \left( \frac{v_{be}}{v_{in}} \right)$$
Substitute the input ratio:
$$A_v = -g_m R_C' \left( \frac{r_\pi}{r_\pi + \beta R_E} \right)$$

Since $g_m = \frac{\beta}{r_\pi}$, we can simplify:
$$A_v = - \left(\frac{\beta}{r_\pi}\right) R_C' \left( \frac{r_\pi}{r_\pi + \beta R_E} \right) = \frac{-\beta R_C'}{r_\pi + \beta R_E}$$

Since $\beta R_E \gg r_\pi$:
$$A_v \approx \frac{-\beta R_C'}{\beta R_E} = -\frac{R_C'}{R_E}$$

Plug in the values:
$$A_v \approx -\frac{1.43\text{k}\Omega}{1\text{k}\Omega} = -1.43$$

**The Joy of Understanding:**
Look at that final formula: $A_v \approx -\frac{R_C'}{R_E}$.
The gain depends *only* on the resistors outside the transistor! It is completely independent of $\beta$, $g_m$, or $r_\pi$.
Why? Because the unbypassed emitter resistor $R_E$ provides **negative feedback**. If the gain tries to spike, more current flows through $R_E$, which drops more voltage, which reduces $v_{be}$, which crushes the gain back down.

By adding a simple $1\text{k}\Omega$ resistor to the emitter, we sacrificed massive gain (it could have been -100+) in exchange for absolute stability. If the transistor heats up and $\beta$ changes from 100 to 200, the gain remains -1.43. This is the fundamental tradeoff of analog design: **Gain vs. Stability**.

*(Note: If we want high gain, we put a capacitor in parallel with $R_E$. The capacitor shorts $R_E$ at AC frequencies, removing the feedback, making $A_v = -g_m R_C'$, but it becomes highly dependent on temperature again. Engineering is all about choosing which lie to tell).*

### S&S / ECA - Laplace & Transfer Functions
*   **s-Impedance:** $Z_R = R$, $Z_L = sL$, $Z_C = \frac{1}{sC}$
*   **Transfer Function:** $H(s) = \frac{Y(s)}{X(s)} = \frac{N(s)}{D(s)}$
*   **Poles:** Roots of $D(s) = 0$. Determine transient response and stability.
*   **Zeros:** Roots of $N(s) = 0$. Determine nulls in frequency response.
*   **Stability Rule:** All poles must have negative real parts ($\sigma < 0$).

### ADE - BJT DC Biasing
*   **Active Region:** $V_{BE} = 0.7\text{V}$, $V_{CE} > 0.3\text{V}$.
*   **Voltage Divider $V_{th}$:** $V_{CC} \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$
*   **Voltage Divider $R_{th}$:** $R_1 \parallel R_2$
*   **Stability Condition:** $(\beta + 1)R_E \ge 10 R_{th}$
*   **Stable $I_E$:** $I_E \approx \frac{V_{th} - 0.7}{R_E}$
*   **Collector Voltage:** $V_{CE} = V_{CC} - I_C(R_C + R_E)$

### ADE - BJT Small Signal (Hybrid-Pi)
*   **Transconductance:** $g_m = \frac{I_{C,Q}}{V_T}$
*   **Base Resistance:** $r_\pi = \frac{\beta}{g_m} = \frac{\beta V_T}{I_{C,Q}}$
*   **AC Output Resistance:** $r_o = \frac{V_A}{I_{C,Q}}$ (Assume $\infty$ if Early voltage $V_A$ not given).
*   **Gain (Unbypassed Emitter):** $A_v \approx -\frac{R_C \parallel R_L}{R_E}$
*   **Gain (Bypassed Emitter):** $A_v \approx -g_m (R_C \parallel R_L)$
*   **Input Resistance (Unbypassed):** $R_{in} = R_1 \parallel R_2 \parallel (r_\pi + (\beta+1)R_E)$
