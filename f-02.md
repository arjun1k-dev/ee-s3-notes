## Day 3: The World of Transients (ECA)

### 1.1 The Physics of the Sudden Jump: Why Transients Exist

If you flip a switch in a purely resistive circuit, the current changes instantly. Resistors don't care about time; they only obey Ohm's law ($v=iR$).

But the universe strictly forbids sudden changes in *stored* energy.
*   A capacitor stores energy in an electric field: $E_C = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$
*   An inductor stores energy in a magnetic field: $E_L = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$

Power is the rate of change of energy: $P = \frac{dE}{dt}$.
If the voltage across a capacitor could change instantly (e.g., from $0\text{V}$ to $5\text{V}$ in zero seconds), then $\frac{dV}{dt} \to \infty$. This means power would be infinite. You cannot have infinite power in a finite space.

> **Physical Reality Callout: The Continuity Rules (The Laws of Physics)**
> 1. **The voltage across a capacitor cannot change instantaneously.** $v_C(0^-) = v_C(0^+)$. If it's $0\text{V}$ before you close the switch, it is *exactly* $0\text{V}$ the millisecond after. Current can spike infinitely (theoretically), but voltage takes time.
> 2. **The current through an inductor cannot change instantaneously.** $i_L(0^-) = i_L(0^+)$. If $2\text{A}$ was flowing before the switch flipped, $2\text{A}$ is flowing the millisecond after. Voltage can spike infinitely (inductive kickback), but current takes time.

### 1.2 Deriving the Universal Transient Template (No Memorizing)

In File 0, we solved the 1st-order ODE: $\frac{dy}{dt} + ay = K$. Let's derive the actual circuit equation so you never have to memorize a formula again.

Take a simple series RC circuit with a step voltage source $V_s$.
By KVL: $V_s = v_R + v_C = iR + v_C$
Since $i = C\frac{dv_C}{dt}$, substitute it in:
$$V_s = RC\frac{dv_C}{dt} + v_C$$

Rearrange into standard ODE form:
$$\frac{dv_C}{dt} + \frac{1}{RC}v_C = \frac{V_s}{RC}$$

Let $\tau = RC$ (we will define this shortly). The equation becomes:
$$\frac{dv_C}{dt} + \frac{1}{\tau}v_C = \frac{V_s}{\tau}$$

**Step 1: Homogeneous Solution (Natural Response)**
Turn off the source ($V_s = 0$):
$$\frac{dv_{C,h}}{dt} + \frac{1}{\tau}v_{C,h} = 0$$
The solution is an exponential decay: $v_{C,h} = A e^{-t/\tau}$, where $A$ is a constant determined by initial conditions.

**Step 2: Particular Solution (Forced Response)**
The input is a constant $V_s$. Guess a constant particular solution $v_{C,p} = K$.
Plug it in: $0 + \frac{1}{\tau}K = \frac{V_s}{\tau} \implies K = V_s$.
So, $v_{C,p} = V_s$. This makes sense! After infinite time, the capacitor charges up to the source voltage.

**Step 3: General Solution**
$$v_C(t) = v_{C,h} + v_{C,p} = A e^{-t/\tau} + V_s$$

**Step 4: Apply Initial Conditions**
At $t = 0^+$, the voltage is $v_C(0^+)$.
$$v_C(0^+) = A e^0 + V_s = A + V_s$$
$$A = v_C(0^+) - V_s$$

**Final Universal Equation:**
Substitute $A$ back into the general solution:
$$v_C(t) = V_s + [v_C(0^+) - V_s]e^{-t/\tau}$$

Replace $V_s$ with the final steady-state value $v_C(\infty)$, and you get the template:
$$x(t) = x_{final} + (x_{initial} - x_{final})e^{-t/\tau}$$

### 1.3 The Hidden Secret of the Time Constant ($\tau$)

$\tau$ (tau) is the **time constant**. It is the heartbeat of the transient.
*   For RC circuits: $\tau = R_{eq}C$
*   For RL circuits: $\tau = \frac{L}{R_{eq}}$

**What is $R_{eq}$?** It is the Thevenin resistance seen by the energy storage element (the C or the L) when you turn off all independent sources and look back into the circuit. *This is why you mastered Thevenin in File 1.*

> **Engineer's Cheat Code: The Tangent Line Trick**
> At $t=0$, draw a tangent line to the exponential curve. That tangent line will intersect the final steady-state value ($x_{final}$) at exactly $t = \tau$.
> If an oscilloscope shows a voltage rising, and you draw a straight line from the initial slope, the time it takes to hit the max voltage is your $\tau$. You don't even need to wait for it to settle to measure it!

> **Engineer's Cheat Code: The 5-Tau Rule**
> After 1 $\tau$, the circuit reaches 63.2% of its journey.
> After 2 $\tau$, it reaches 86.5%.
> After 3 $\tau$, it reaches 95.0%.
> After 5 $\tau$, it reaches 99.3%.
> In engineering, we don't wait for infinity. **If $t \ge 5\tau$, the transient is dead.** Write this in pen on your brain.

### 1.4 The "Delicacy" Problem: Source-Free RC Transient

Let's apply this to a circuit that has *no source*. It relies entirely on stored energy.

**The Setup:**
A $2\mu\text{F}$ capacitor has been charged to $12\text{V}$. At $t=0$, a switch connects it to a $100\text{k}\Omega$ resistor. Find the voltage and current at $t=0.2\text{s}$.

```text
       Switch closes at t=0
         |
         o----||----> + 12V (initially)
         |    2uF
        [R] 100kΩ
         |
        GND
```

**The Grind:**
1.  **Find initial values:** $v_C(0^+) = 12\text{V}$.
2.  **Find final values:** $v_C(\infty) = 0\text{V}$ (There is no source to keep it charged; the resistor will dissipate all the energy as heat).
3.  **Find $\tau$:** $\tau = RC = (100 \times 10^3)(2 \times 10^{-6}) = 200 \times 10^{-3} = 0.2\text{ seconds}$.
4.  **Apply Template:**
    $$v_C(t) = 0 + (12 - 0)e^{-t/0.2} = 12e^{-5t} \text{ V}$$
5.  **Calculate at $t=0.2\text{s}$:**
    Notice that $t=0.2\text{s}$ is exactly $1\tau$.
    $$v_C(0.2) = 12e^{-1} \approx 12(0.368) = 4.41\text{ V}$$

**What about the current?**
Do not use the template for current if you can avoid it; use the capacitor equation.
$i_C(t) = C\frac{dv_C}{dt} = C \frac{d}{dt}(12e^{-5t}) = 2\mu\text{F} \cdot 12 \cdot (-5)e^{-5t} = -120e^{-5t} \mu\text{A}$.

> **Physical Reality Callout:**
> Why is the current negative? Because the capacitor is discharging. Conventional current is flowing *out* of the positive terminal, opposite to the passive sign convention we used to define it. The magnitude at $t=0$ is $120\mu\text{A}$. It takes $5 \times 0.2 = 1\text{ second}$ for the capacitor to fully dump its energy into the resistor as heat.

### 1.5 The "Delicacy" Problem: Step Response via Thevenin

Let's combine Day 1's Thevenin theorem with Day 3's transients. This is the ultimate "industry-grade" approach to solving first-order circuits. Never use KCL/KVL for a first-order transient if you can use Thevenin. It saves too much time.

**The Setup:**
A $24\text{V}$ source is in series with a $6\Omega$ resistor. This feeds a node. Connected to that node is a $3\Omega$ resistor to ground, and a $2\Omega$ resistor in series with a $0.5\text{F}$ capacitor to ground. The switch closes at $t=0$. The capacitor is initially uncharged. Find $v_C(t)$ for $t>0$.

```text
      + 24V -
        |
       [6Ω]
        |
        o-------+-------+-----> Node A
        |       |       |
       [3Ω]    [2Ω]    === 0.5F (C)
        |       |       |
       GND     GND     GND
```

**The Grind (The Thevenin Way):**

**Step 1: Find $V_{th}$ (The final value of the capacitor).**
Disconnect the capacitor. What is the open-circuit voltage at Node A?
The $3\Omega$ and the invisible infinite resistance of the open capacitor terminals form a voltage divider.
$V_{th} = V_A = 24\text{V} \times \frac{3\Omega}{6\Omega + 3\Omega} = 24 \times \frac{3}{9} = 8\text{V}$.
*Since the cap will charge up to this voltage, $v_C(\infty) = 8\text{V}$.*

**Step 2: Find $R_{th}$ (To find $\tau$).**
Turn off the $24\text{V}$ source (short it to ground). Look into the terminals where the capacitor was.
The $6\Omega$ is in parallel with the $3\Omega$.
$R_{eq} = 6\Omega \parallel 3\Omega = \frac{6 \times 3}{6 + 3} = 2\Omega$.
Add the series $2\Omega$ resistor: $R_{th} = 2\Omega + 2\Omega = 4\Omega$.

**Step 3: Calculate $\tau$.**
$\tau = R_{th}C = (4\Omega)(0.5\text{F}) = 2\text{ seconds}$.

**Step 4: Apply the Universal Template.**
$v_C(t) = v_C(\infty) + [v_C(0^+) - v_C(\infty)]e^{-t/\tau}$
$v_C(t) = 8 + [0 - 8]e^{-t/2}$
$$v_C(t) = 8 - 8e^{-t/2} \text{ Volts}$$

**The Joy of Understanding:**
Look at what we just did. We took a multi-node circuit with a switch, and without writing a single differential equation, we predicted its exact behavior for all of time. Thevenin reduced the entire universe to a simple battery ($8\text{V}$), a resistor ($4\Omega$), and a capacitor ($0.5\text{F}$).

### 1.6 The "Delicacy" Problem: RL Circuit Transient

Inductors behave symmetrically to capacitors, but we solve for *current* first, not voltage.

**The Setup:**
A $10\text{V}$ source, a $2\Omega$ resistor, and a $5\text{H}$ inductor are in series. Switch closes at $t=0$. Find $i_L(t)$ and $v_L(t)$.

**The Grind:**
1.  **Initial:** $i_L(0^+) = 0\text{A}$ (inductor current cannot jump).
2.  **Final:** $i_L(\infty) = \frac{10\text{V}}{2\Omega} = 5\text{A}$ (Inductor acts as a short circuit in steady-state DC).
3.  **$\tau$:** $\tau = \frac{L}{R} = \frac{5}{2} = 2.5\text{ seconds}$.
4.  **Apply Template:**
    $$i_L(t) = 5 + (0 - 5)e^{-t/2.5} = 5(1 - e^{-0.4t}) \text{ A}$$

**Finding Voltage across Inductor:**
Do NOT use the template for $v_L$. Use KVL or the inductor equation.
$v_L(t) = L\frac{di_L}{dt} = 5 \frac{d}{dt}[5(1 - e^{-0.4t})] = 25(0.4e^{-0.4t}) = 10e^{-0.4t} \text{ V}$.

> **Physical Reality Callout:**
> At $t=0^+$, the inductor current is $0$, so it acts as an *open circuit*. All $10\text{V}$ drops across the inductor. Our formula confirms: $v_L(0) = 10e^0 = 10\text{V}$. As time goes on, the magnetic field builds, the current rises, and the inductor's opposition ($v_L$) decays to $0\text{V}$.

## Day 3: Hour 3 - Signal Classification & System Properties (S&S)

Before we process signals, we must classify them. The most important classification is **Energy vs. Power**.

### 2.1 Energy vs. Power Signals: The Ultimate Litmus Test

Why do we care? Because in communications, we need to know if we are transmitting a finite burst of data (energy) or a continuous broadcast (power). The math dictates the hardware design (e.g., antenna sizing, amplifier biasing).

*   **Total Energy ($E$):** The total energy delivered to a $1\Omega$ resistor. $E = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |x(t)|^2 dt$
    *   *Note:* We use $|x(t)|^2$ instead of $x^2(t)$ because signals can be complex (in S&S, we deal with complex exponentials all the time).
*   **Average Power ($P$):** The energy delivered per unit time. $P = \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{2T} \int_{-T}^{T} |x(t)|^2 dt$

**The Strict Rules:**
1.  **Energy Signal:** $0 < E < \infty$, and therefore $P = 0$. (Examples: A single rectangular pulse, a decaying exponential $e^{-t}u(t)$).
2.  **Power Signal:** $0 < P < \infty$, and therefore $E = \infty$. (Examples: A sine wave, a DC constant, a periodic square wave).
3.  **Neither:** $E = \infty$ and $P = \infty$. (Example: $x(t) = t$. A ramp that grows forever).

### 2.2 The Weird Edge Cases of Energy/Power

Let's test your understanding. Classify the unit step function $u(t)$.
*   $E = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |u(t)|^2 dt = \int_{0}^{\infty} 1 dt = \infty$.
*   $P = \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{2T} \int_{-T}^{T} |u(t)|^2 dt = \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{2T} \int_{0}^{T} 1 dt = \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{T}{2T} = \frac{1}{2}$.
*   **Result:** The unit step function is a **Power Signal** ($P = 0.5\text{W}$). Most students guess it's an Energy signal because it "starts" at zero. But it goes on forever, so energy is infinite, but average power is finite!

### 2.3 LTI System Properties: Causality and BIBO Stability

We defined Linearity and Time-Invariance earlier. Now, two properties that dictate if a system can actually be built in the real world.

**1. Causality:**
A system is causal if the output at time $t$ depends *only* on present and past inputs.
*   $y(t) = x(t-1)$ (Causal - delays the signal)
*   $y(t) = x(t+1)$ (Non-Causal - sees the future!)
*   *Impulse Response Test:* A system is causal **if and only if** $h(t) = 0$ for all $t < 0$. If the system rings *before* you hit it, it's non-causal. You cannot build a non-causal system in hardware without using time-delay buffers.

**2. BIBO Stability (Bounded-Input Bounded-Output):**
If you feed a finite signal into the system, it must produce a finite output. If you sing into a microphone and the speaker blows up (feedback loop), the system is unstable.
*   *Impulse Response Test:* An LTI system is BIBO stable **if and only if** the area under the impulse response is finite:
    $$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |h(t)| dt < \infty$$
*   If $h(t) = e^{-t}u(t)$, the area is 1. (Stable).
*   If $h(t) = e^{t}u(t)$, the area goes to infinity. (Unstable).

## Day 4: LTI Systems & The Convolution Integral (S&S)

### 3.1 Deriving the Convolution Integral (The Sifting Property)

Students freeze when they see $y(t) = \int x(\tau)h(t-\tau) d\tau$. Let's derive it so it becomes obvious.

**Step 1: Represent any signal as a sum of impulses.**
By the Sifting Property of the Dirac Delta function, any signal can be written as:
$$x(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(\tau)\delta(t-\tau) d\tau$$
This means $x(t)$ is just an infinite sum of shifted, scaled impulses. At time $\tau$, there is an impulse of height $x(\tau)$.

**Step 2: Apply the LTI properties.**
If the input to an LTI system is an impulse $\delta(t)$, the output is $h(t)$.
By Time-Invariance, if the input is $\delta(t-\tau)$, the output is $h(t-\tau)$.
By Linearity (Homogeneity), if the input is $x(\tau)\delta(t-\tau)$, the output is $x(\tau)h(t-\tau)$.

**Step 3: Add them all up.**
Since the total input $x(t)$ is the integral (sum) of all those tiny impulses, the total output $y(t)$ must be the integral (sum) of all their individual responses:
$$y(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(\tau)h(t-\tau) d\tau$$

**The Joy of Understanding:**
Convolution is not a magical new math operation. It is literally just "chop the input into pieces, find the output for each piece using $h(t)$, and add them all up." The $t-\tau$ is just accounting for the time delay of each piece.

### 3.2 The "Delicacy" Problem: Graphical Convolution Decoded (Exp vs Pulse)

Let's compute $y(t) = x(t) * h(t)$.
Let $x(t) = u(t) - u(t-2)$ (A rectangular pulse of height 1 from $t=0$ to $t=2$).
Let $h(t) = e^{-t}u(t)$ (A standard decaying exponential).

**The Grind (Step-by-Step Graphical Method):**
The variable of integration is $\tau$ (fake time). The variable $t$ is the real time we are evaluating.

1.  **Draw $x(\tau)$:** A rectangle sitting on the $\tau$ axis from $0$ to $2$.
2.  **Draw $h(-\tau)$:** Take $h(\tau)$ (exponential starting at $\tau=0$ going right) and FLIP it across the y-axis. Now it starts at $\tau=0$ going left.
3.  **Slide $h(t-\tau)$:** Shift the flipped exponential by $t$.

**Region 1: $t < 0$ (No Overlap)**
The flipped exponential is to the left of the origin. The rectangle is to the right.
**$y(t) = 0$**

**Region 2: $0 \le t < 2$ (Partial Overlap)**
The flipped exponential is sliding into the rectangle. The overlap starts at $\tau=0$ and ends at $\tau=t$.
$$y(t) = \int_{0}^{t} (1) \cdot e^{-(t-\tau)} d\tau$$
Notice $e^{-t}$ is constant with respect to $d\tau$, pull it out:
$$y(t) = e^{-t} \int_{0}^{t} e^{\tau} d\tau = e^{-t} \left[ e^{\tau} \right]_0^t = e^{-t} (e^t - 1)$$
$$y(t) = 1 - e^{-t}$$

**Region 3: $t \ge 2$ (Full Overlap, Sliding Out)**
The exponential has completely passed through the rectangle. The overlap is locked from $\tau=0$ to $\tau=2$.
$$y(t) = \int_{0}^{2} (1) \cdot e^{-(t-\tau)} d\tau = e^{-t} \int_{0}^{2} e^{\tau} d\tau$$
$$y(t) = e^{-t} \left[ e^{\tau} \right]_0^2 = e^{-t} (e^2 - 1) = e^{-(t-2)} - e^{-t}$$

### 3.3 The "Delicacy" Problem: Convolution of Two Rectangles (Triangle Generation)

This is a classic PYQ pattern. Convolving a rectangle with itself.

Let $x(t) = u(t) - u(t-1)$ (Pulse from 0 to 1).
Let $h(t) = u(t) - u(t-1)$ (Pulse from 0 to 1).
Find $y(t) = x(t) * h(t)$.

**The Grind:**
1.  **Region 1 ($t < 0$):** $y(t) = 0$
2.  **Region 2 ($0 \le t < 1$):** Overlap from $0$ to $t$. Height is $1 \times 1 = 1$.
    $y(t) = \int_0^t 1 d\tau = t$. (A ramp going up).
3.  **Region 3 ($1 \le t < 2$):** The sliding pulse is exiting. Overlap is from $t-1$ to $1$. Length of overlap is $1 - (t-1) = 2-t$.
    $y(t) = \int_{t-1}^1 1 d\tau = 1 - (t-1) = 2-t$. (A ramp going down).
4.  **Region 4 ($t \ge 2$):** $y(t) = 0$.

**The Joy of Understanding:**
Convolving a square pulse with a square pulse creates a **Triangle pulse**. In digital communications, this is exactly how we shape signals to avoid Inter-Symbol Interference (ISI) using raised cosine filters, which are essentially smoothed triangles. Convolution acts as a geometric smoothing operator.

### 3.4 Convolution vs. Correlation: Don't Mix Them Up

*   **Convolution:** $x(t) * h(t) = \int x(\tau)h(t-\tau) d\tau$. One signal is *flipped*. Used to find the output of an LTI system.
*   **Cross-Correlation:** $R_{xy}(\tau) = \int x(t) y(t+\tau) dt$. Signals are *shifted, not flipped*. Used in radar and signal processing to find a pattern (like a radar chirp) buried in noise. If the correlation spikes, the pattern is present.

## Day 4: Hour 3 - Karnaugh Maps & Hardware Realities (Digital/ADE)

### 4.1 The Geometry of Logic and Gray Code

In File 1, we used Boolean algebra. But algebra is sequential and error-prone. Maurice Karnaugh invented the K-Map to turn Boolean algebra into a spatial puzzle.

**The Golden Rule of K-Maps: Adjacency.**
In a K-Map, only **one variable can change** when moving horizontally or vertically between adjacent cells. This is **Gray Code** (00, 01, 11, 10), NOT binary code (00, 01, 10, 11).

> **Physical Reality Callout: The Hardware Glitch (Static Hazard)**
> Why Gray code? Imagine a binary counter going from 01 to 10. Both bits change at once. In real silicon, the top transistor might switch a nanosecond faster than the bottom one. For that nanosecond, the system sees 00 or 11—a false state. This causes a "glitch" or "static hazard" that can trigger a false clock pulse and crash a CPU. Gray code guarantees only ONE bit changes at a time, eliminating the glitch.

### 4.2 Static Hazards: When Boolean Math Lies to Hardware

You minimized $Y = AB + \bar{A}C$ using Boolean algebra. It's mathematically perfect.
Let $B=1, C=1$. Then $Y = A + \bar{A} = 1$.
If $A$ switches from 1 to 0, math says $Y$ should stay 1 forever.
But in hardware, the gate for $AB$ turns off a nanosecond before the gate for $\bar{A}C$ turns on. For that nanosecond, $Y = 0$. **A false zero glitch.**

**The Fix:** Add a redundant term that covers the transition. The transition happened where $B=1$ and $C=1$. So add the term $BC$.
$Y = AB + \bar{A}C + BC$.
Mathematically, $BC$ is useless (it's absorbed). Physically, it holds the output HIGH during the transition. K-Maps naturally prevent this if you group overlapping prime implicants correctly.

### 4.3 The "Delicacy" Problem: 4-Variable K-Map with Don't Cares

**The Setup:**
A digital system has inputs A, B, C, D. The output $F$ is 1 for minterms: 0, 2, 5, 7, 8, 10, 13, 15.
The input combinations 4, 6, and 14 are physically impossible in this system (Don't Cares - $X$).
Minimize $F$.

**The Grind:**
Draw the 4x4 K-Map.

```text
        CD
      \ 00  01  11  10
    AB +---+---+---+---+
    00 | 1 | 0 | 0 | 1 |  (Minterms 0, 2)
       +---+---+---+---+
    01 | X | 1 | 1 | X |  (Minterms 5,7; X at 4,6)
       +---+---+---+---+
    11 | 0 | 1 | 1 | X |  (Minterms 13,15; X at 14)
       +---+---+---+---+
    10 | 1 | 0 | 0 | 1 |  (Minterms 8, 10)
       +---+---+---+---+
```

**Grouping Strategy:**
1.  **Group the 1s and Xs to make the largest powers of 2.**
2.  *Group 1 (Quad):* Look at the four corners (minterms 0, 2, 8, 10). They form a 4-cell group. What do they have in common? $C=0$ and $D=0$? No, look at the edges. A and C are changing. B=0, D=0. Wait, top-left and top-right have B=0, D=0. Bottom-left and bottom-right have B=0, D=0. Yes! Group = $\bar{B}\bar{D}$.
3.  *Group 2 (Quad):* Look at the middle-right column (minterms 5, 7, 13, 15). Wait, 5 and 7 are in the 01 row. 13 and 15 are in the 11 row. This is a vertical quad spanning AB=01 and AB=11. What's constant? A=1 (since 01 and 11 both have A=1... wait, 01 means A=0. Let's re-verify: 01 is A=0, B=1. 11 is A=1, B=1. So B=1 is constant. C=1, D is changing (01 and 11). So this quad is $BC$.
4.  *Group 3 (Quad):* Look at minterms 4, 5, 6, 7 (the entire 01 row). Even though 4 and 6 are X, we can use them! The 01 row means A=0, B=1. So this group is $\bar{A}B$.
5.  *Group 4 (Quad):* Look at minterms 4, 6, 14, 15... wait. Let's look at the remaining 1s. Are all 1s covered?
    *   0, 2, 8, 10 covered by $\bar{B}\bar{D}$.
    *   5, 7, 13, 15 covered by $BC$.
    *   All 1s are covered!

**The Final Logic:**
Do we need Group 3 ($\bar{A}B$)? No, because all the 1s in that row (5, 7) are already covered by the $BC$ group. Adding $\bar{A}B$ would be redundant logic. We only add redundant terms if we need to fix a static hazard (which we don't see here because the transitions are covered by the existing quads).

**Result:** $F = \bar{B}\bar{D} + BC$

**The Joy of Understanding:**
We started with a messy truth table of 8 required states and 3 impossible states. By utilizing the impossible states as "free real estate" to build larger geometric blocks, we reduced a massive sum-of-products into just two simple AND gates and one OR gate. That means faster silicon, less power, and cheaper manufacturing.

### ECA - Transients
*   **Continuity:** $v_C(0^+) = v_C(0^-)$ and $i_L(0^+) = i_L(0^-)$
*   **Universal Equation:** $x(t) = x(\infty) + [x(0^+) - x(\infty)]e^{-t/\tau}$
*   **Time Constants:** $\tau_{RC} = R_{th}C$, $\tau_{RL} = L / R_{th}$
*   **5-Tau Rule:** Steady state reached at $t = 5\tau$.
*   **Initial Slope:** The tangent at $t=0$ hits $x(\infty)$ at $t=\tau$.
*   **Capacitor Current:** $i_C = C\frac{dv_C}{dt}$ (Do not use template for this).
*   **Inductor Voltage:** $v_L = L\frac{di_L}{dt}$ (Do not use template for this).

### S&S - Signals
*   **Energy:** $E = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |x(t)|^2 dt$
*   **Power:** $P = \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{2T} \int_{-T}^{T} |x(t)|^2 dt$
*   **Periodic Power:** $P = \frac{1}{T} \int_{0}^{T} |x(t)|^2 dt$
*   **Unit Step Power:** $P_{u(t)} = 0.5\text{W}$

### S&S - LTI Systems & Convolution
*   **Convolution Integral:** $y(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(\tau)h(t-\tau) d\tau$
*   **Properties:** Commutative ($x*h = h*x$), Associative $((x*h)*g = x*(h*g))$, Distributive ($x*(h+g) = x*h + x*g)$.
*   **Identity:** $x(t) * \delta(t) = x(t)$
*   **Shift:** $x(t-t_1) * \delta(t-t_2) = x(t - t_1 - t_2)$
*   **Causality Test:** $h(t) = 0$ for $t < 0$.
*   **Stability Test (BIBO):** $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |h(t)| dt < \infty$.

### Digital - K-Maps
*   **3-Var Order:** $000, 001, 011, 010$ (top) | $100, 101, 111, 110$ (bottom).
*   **4-Var Order:** Add $11, 10$ to the right of 3-var.
*   **Grouping:** Powers of 2 (1, 2, 4, 8, 16). Wrap-around allowed.
*   **Don't Cares ($X$):** Use as 1 if it makes a group larger; ignore if not needed. Do not leave an $X$ ungrouped if it can help.
