## Day 5: Diodes - The One-Way Valve & Waveform Shapers (ADE)

### 1.1 The Physics of the PN Junction (Breaking Ohm's Law)

A resistor has a linear I-V curve: $I = V/R$.
A diode is made by fusing P-type (holes) and N-type (electrons) silicon. At the boundary, they annihilate each other, creating a "depletion region" void of free charge. This region acts as an insulating wall with an internal electric field.

To get current to flow, you must push hard enough to overcome this internal wall. The Shockley Diode Equation governs this:
$$I_D = I_S \left( e^{V_D / \eta V_T} - 1 \right)$$
*   $I_S$: Saturation current (tiny, $\sim 10^{-12}\text{A}$).
*   $V_T$: Thermal voltage ($\approx 26\text{mV}$ at room temp).
*   $\eta$: Ideality factor (1 for silicon, 2 for germanium).

> **Physical Reality Callout: The Exponential Wall**
> Look at the equation. Because it's inside an exponent, a tiny change in voltage causes a massive change in current. If $V_D = 0.6\text{V}$, almost no current flows. If $V_D = 0.7\text{V}$, milliamperes flow. If $V_D = 0.8\text{V}$, the diode melts and explodes. You cannot put a voltage source directly across a diode; it will act as a dead short and self-destruct. A diode is a **current-controlled** device that we *model* with voltage.

### 1.2 The Engineer's Lie: Constant Voltage Drop Model

Solving $I = I_S(e^{V/0.026} - 1)$ inside a circuit with KVL is mathematically horrific (it requires iterative Newton-Raphson methods).

In industry, we lie. We linearize the curve. We look at the exponential bend (usually around $0.7\text{V}$ for Silicon) and draw a straight tangent line.
**The Rule:** If a diode is forward-biased (conducting), pretend it is a $0.7\text{V}$ battery. If it is reverse-biased (blocking), pretend it is an open circuit.

> **Engineer's Cheat Code: The "Assume and Verify" Method**
> 1. **Assume** a state for the diode (ON or OFF).
> 2. Replace the diode with its model ($0.7\text{V}$ battery or open circuit).
> 3. Solve the circuit using normal linear KVL/KCL.
> 4. **Verify:** Check the current through the battery model. If you assumed ON and got positive current, you are correct. If you assumed ON and got *negative* current, your assumption was wrong (diodes can't conduct backwards). Redo assuming OFF.

### 1.3 The "Delicacy" Problem: Multi-Level Asymmetric Clipping

Clippers are circuits that chop off parts of a waveform. They are used to protect inputs (e.g., limiting voltage to an ADC pin) or generate square waves from sine waves.

**The Setup:**
We have an input sinusoid $v_i = 10\sin(\omega t)$. We want to clip the positive peak at $+4\text{V}$ and the negative peak at $-2\text{V}$.
Design the circuit using Silicon diodes and batteries.

**The Architecture:**
To clip the positive peak, we need a diode pointing *towards* the output, with a $4\text{V}$ battery.
To clip the negative peak, we need a diode pointing *away* from the output, with a $2\text{V}$ battery.

```text
           +4V                -2V
            |                  |
           [+] D1 (Anode)    [-] D2 (Cathode)
            |                  |
vi ---o-----+------------------+-----o--- vo
      |                         |
     GND                       GND
```
*Wait, let's trace the polarities perfectly.*
For D1 to turn ON during the positive peak, the anode must be higher than the cathode.
Anode is at $v_i$. Cathode is connected to $+4\text{V}$.
D1 turns ON when $v_i > 4\text{V}$. When ON, $v_o = v_i - 0.7\text{V}$? No, if it's an ideal battery model, $v_o$ is clamped to $4\text{V} + 0.7\text{V} = 4.7\text{V}$.
*Correction for exact $4\text{V}$ clipping:* We must account for the diode drop! To clip at exactly $4\text{V}$, the battery must be $V_{clip} - 0.7\text{V}$.
So, Battery 1 = $4.0 - 0.7 = 3.3\text{V}$. Battery 2 = $|-2.0| - 0.7 = 1.3\text{V}$.

```text
          +3.3V               +1.3V
            |                  |
           [+] D1              [-] D2
            |                  |
vi ---o-----+------------------+-----o--- vo
```

**The Grind (Tracing the Output):**
1.  **When $-1.3\text{V} < v_i < +3.3\text{V}$:**
    Both diodes are reverse-biased. No current flows through the branches.
    $v_o = v_i$. (The sine wave passes perfectly).
2.  **When $v_i > +3.3\text{V}$ (Positive Clip):**
    D1 becomes forward-biased. It acts as a $0.7\text{V}$ battery.
    KVL: $v_i = V_{bat1} + V_{D1} + v_o \implies v_i = 3.3\text{V} + 0.7\text{V} + v_o \implies v_o = v_i - 4.0\text{V}$.
    *Wait, that means as $v_i$ goes to $10\text{V}$, $v_o$ goes to $6\text{V}$? NO.*
    *Ah! The diode clamps the node.* If $v_o$ tried to go above $4.0\text{V}$, D1 would short it out. The maximum $v_o$ can reach is $4.0\text{V}$. The excess voltage drops across the series resistance of the source (which I omitted for simplicity, but in reality, there is always an $R_{source}$ or a series resistor to prevent infinite current).
    Let's add the required series resistor $R$ to make the math rigorous.

```text
          +3.3V               +1.3V
            |                  |
           [+] D1              [-] D2
            |                  |
vi ---[R]---o------------------+-----o--- vo
            |                  |
           GND                GND
```

Now trace it:
*   **Positive Clip:** When $v_i > 3.3\text{V}$, D1 shorts the output node to the $3.3\text{V}$ battery plus the $0.7\text{V}$ diode drop. $v_o$ is rigidly clamped at **$4.0\text{V}$**. The rest of the voltage drops across $R$.
*   **Negative Clip:** When $v_i < -1.3\text{V}$, D2 conducts. The output is clamped to $-1.3\text{V} - 0.7\text{V} = $ **$-2.0\text{V}$**.
*   **Passband:** Between $-1.3\text{V}$ and $+3.3\text{V}$, diodes are open, $v_o = v_i$.

**The Joy of Understanding:**
You just built a voltage limiter. This exact topology is sitting inside every microcontroller pin right now. If a static shock hits the pin at $50\text{V}$, the internal ESD diodes clamp the voltage to $V_{cc} + 0.7\text{V}$, routing the destructive energy safely to the power rails and saving the $5 silicon die.

### 1.4 The "Delicacy" Problem: The DC Restorer (Clamping Circuits)

While clippers *chop* the waveform, clampers *shift* the entire waveform up or down. They add a DC component to an AC signal without changing its shape. They are also known as DC Restorers because they restore the DC component lost when a signal passes through a capacitor (AC coupling).

**The Setup:**
A positive clamper. Input is a $10\text{V}$ peak sine wave ($-10\text{V}$ to $+10\text{V}$). We want to shift it entirely above zero (from $0\text{V}$ to $20\text{V}$).

**The Architecture:**
A capacitor in series with the signal, and a diode in parallel with the output, pointing down to ground.

```text
vi ---||---o-------+--- vo
     C    |       |
          +---[-] | (Cathode up, Anode down)
               D  |
                  |
                 GND
```

**The Grind (The Physics of the Shift):**
How does the capacitor "know" to shift the DC level? It doesn't. It relies on the asymmetry of the diode.

**Phase 1: The Negative Half-Cycle (Charging)**
When $v_i$ swings to $-10\text{V}$, the diode is forward-biased (current flows down to ground).
The capacitor charges. To what voltage?
KVL loop: $v_i - V_C - V_D = 0 \implies -10\text{V} - V_C - 0.7\text{V} = 0 \implies V_C = -10.7\text{V}$.
*Wait, the top plate of the cap is negative relative to the bottom plate.* The capacitor has hoarded a massive $10.7\text{V}$ charge.

**Phase 2: The Positive Half-Cycle (Clamping)**
When $v_i$ swings to $+10\text{V}$, the diode is reverse-biased (open circuit). No discharge path exists (assuming no load resistor).
What is the output voltage?
KVL: $v_o = v_i - V_C = 10\text{V} - (-10.7\text{V}) = 20.7\text{V}$.

**The Steady State:**
If we account for the $0.7\text{V}$ diode drop during the negative peak, the output sits at $-0.7\text{V}$ at the bottom, and swings up to $+20.7\text{V}$ at the top.
*To make it ideal (0V to 20V), we put a DC supply in series with the diode to cancel the 0.7V drop, but in practice, we usually just accept the $-0.7\text{V}$ offset.*

> **Engineer's Cheat Code: The Clamping Rule of Thumb**
> A diode pointing DOWN clamps the negative peak to $-0.7\text{V}$ (shifts waveform UP).
> A diode pointing UP clamps the positive peak to $+0.7\text{V}$ (shifts waveform DOWN).
> The capacitor charges to the peak-to-peak voltage of the input signal ($V_{pp}$).

## Day 5: Hour 3 - Series & Parallel Resonance (ECA)

### 2.1 The Physics of Resonance: Energy Sloshing

Resonance is one of the most beautiful phenomena in physics. It is the reason a wine glass shatters when a singer hits the right note, and the reason you tune a radio.

In an RLC circuit:
*   The Inductor stores energy in a magnetic field: $E_L = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$
*   The Capacitor stores energy in an electric field: $E_C = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$

At resonance, the inductor and capacitor continuously trade energy back and forth—like water sloshing in a bathtub. The magnetic field collapses, building the electric field. The electric field collapses, building the magnetic field.

> **Physical Reality Callout: The Source Goes on Vacation**
> Because the energy is perfectly trapped between L and C, the external voltage source doesn't have to provide any *net* reactive power. It only has to supply the tiny amount of real power dissipated by the resistor (friction). At exact resonance, the total impedance of a series RLC circuit becomes purely resistive ($Z = R$). The imaginary parts (the $+j$ and $-j$) completely annihilate each other.

**The Resonant Frequency ($\omega_0$):**
This is the exact frequency where the inductive reactance equals the capacitive reactance.
$$\omega_0 L = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C} \implies \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$$
*(Note: This formula is identical for BOTH series and parallel resonance, provided the parallel circuit is ideal).*

### 2.2 Quality Factor (Q) and Bandwidth

How "sharp" is the resonance? A tuning fork has a very sharp resonance (high Q). A lump of jello has a broad resonance (low Q).

**Quality Factor ($Q$)** is defined as the ratio of stored energy to dissipated energy per cycle:
$$Q = 2\pi \frac{\text{Peak Stored Energy}}{\text{Energy Dissipated per cycle}}$$

**For a Series RLC:**
At resonance, the current is maximum: $I = V/R$.
Voltage across inductor: $V_L = I \cdot \omega_0 L = \frac{V}{R} \cdot \omega_0 L$.
Therefore, $Q_s = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R}$.

**For a Parallel RLC:**
At resonance, the voltage is maximum: $V = IR$.
Current through inductor: $I_L = \frac{V}{\omega_0 L} = \frac{IR}{\omega_0 L}$.
Therefore, $Q_p = \frac{R}{\omega_0 L}$. *(Notice the R is in the numerator now!)*

**Bandwidth ($BW$):**
This is the range of frequencies where the circuit responds strongly. It is measured between the "Half-Power Frequencies" ($\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$), where the power drops to 50% (which means voltage/current drops to $1/\sqrt{2} \approx 0.707$ of its maximum).
$$BW = \omega_2 - \omega_1 = \frac{\omega_0}{Q}$$

> **Engineer's Cheat Code:**
> High $Q$ means narrow Bandwidth. Low $Q$ means wide Bandwidth. If you are building a radio to pick up exactly 98.1 MHz and block 98.3 MHz, you need a massive $Q$ factor.

### 2.3 The "Delicacy" Problem: Finding the Half-Power Frequencies

**The Setup:**
A Series RLC circuit has $R = 10\Omega$, $L = 10\text{mH}$, and $C = 1\mu\text{F}$.
Find the resonant frequency ($\omega_0$), the Quality factor ($Q_s$), the Bandwidth ($BW$), and the exact half-power frequencies ($\omega_1, \omega_2$).

**The Grind:**
**1. Resonant Frequency:**
$\omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(10 \times 10^{-3})(1 \times 10^{-6})}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{10^{-8}}} = \frac{1}{10^{-4}} = 10,000 \text{ rad/s}$.

**2. Quality Factor:**
$Q_s = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{(10^4)(10 \times 10^{-3})}{10} = \frac{100}{10} = 10$.
*(A Q of 10 is very sharp!)*

**3. Bandwidth:**
$BW = \frac{\omega_0}{Q_s} = \frac{10,000}{10} = 1,000 \text{ rad/s}$.

**4. Half-Power Frequencies:**
We know $\omega_2 - \omega_1 = 1,000$.
We also know a fundamental geometric property of resonance: $\omega_0 = \sqrt{\omega_1 \omega_2}$.
So, $10,000 = \sqrt{\omega_1 \omega_2} \implies \omega_1 \omega_2 = 10^8$.

Now we have a system of equations:
1) $\omega_2 - \omega_1 = 1000$
2) $\omega_1 \omega_2 = 100,000,000$

From (1), $\omega_2 = \omega_1 + 1000$. Substitute into (2):
$\omega_1 (\omega_1 + 1000) = 100,000,000$
$\omega_1^2 + 1000\omega_1 - 100,000,000 = 0$

Use the quadratic formula ($\omega_1 = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$):
$\omega_1 = \frac{-1000 \pm \sqrt{1000^2 - 4(1)(-10^8)}}{2} = \frac{-1000 \pm \sqrt{10^6 + 4 \times 10^8}}{2}$
$\omega_1 = \frac{-1000 \pm \sqrt{401,000,000}}{2} \approx \frac{-1000 \pm 20025}{2}$

Take the positive root:
$\omega_1 = \frac{19025}{2} = 9512.5 \text{ rad/s}$ (Lower cutoff).
$\omega_2 = 9512.5 + 1000 = 10512.5 \text{ rad/s}$ (Upper cutoff).

**The Joy of Understanding:**
You just mathematically defined the "passband" of a filter. Any frequency between $9512$ and $10512$ rad/s will pass through this circuit with less than 3dB of attenuation. Anything outside that range will be brutally suppressed. You have designed a bandpass filter using pure algebra and physics.

## Day 6: Fourier Series - Decomposing Reality (S&S)

### 3.1 Why Fourier? The LTI Loophole for Non-Sinusoids

In File 1, we learned that phasors only work if the input is a *single sinusoid*. But what if the input is a square wave? A triangle wave? A speech signal?

**Joseph Fourier's Genius Insight:**
*Any periodic signal, no matter how ugly or jagged, can be perfectly reconstructed by adding together an infinite number of pure sine and cosine waves at different frequencies.*

Why is this the greatest loophole in engineering? Because if we can break a square wave into 100 sine waves, and we know our circuit is LTI (Linear), we can just use phasors to find the output for *each* of the 100 sine waves individually, and then add the outputs back together at the end! We bypassed the non-sinusoidal restriction.

> **Physical Reality Callout: Why does a square wave sound "buzzy"?**
> A $1\text{kHz}$ square wave is mathematically constructed from a $1\text{kHz}$ sine wave (the fundamental), plus a $3\text{kHz}$ sine wave, plus a $5\text{kHz}$ sine wave, plus all odd harmonics to infinity. When you play a square wave through a speaker, you are physically pushing the speaker cone with thousands of different frequencies at once. The high-frequency harmonics cause the cone to vibrate violently, creating the harsh, buzzing sound.

### 3.2 Trigonometric vs. Exponential Forms

**Trigonometric Form (The Intuitive Way):**
$$x(t) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[ a_n \cos(n\omega_0 t) + b_n \sin(n\omega_0 t) \right]$$
*   $a_0$: The DC average.
*   $a_n, b_n$: The amplitudes of the cosine and sine harmonics.
*   $\omega_0 = \frac{2\pi}{T}$: The fundamental frequency.

**Exponential Form (The Engineer's Way):**
Using Euler's identity ($\cos\theta = \frac{e^{j\theta} + e^{-j\theta}}{2}$), we can combine $a_n$ and $b_n$ into a single complex coefficient $c_n$.
$$x(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{jn\omega_0 t}$$
Where the coefficient is found by:
$$c_n = \frac{1}{T} \int_{0}^{T} x(t) e^{-jn\omega_0 t} dt$$

> **Engineer's Cheat Code:**
> The exponential form is vastly superior for calculations. Instead of calculating two integrals ($a_n$ and $b_n$), you calculate one integral ($c_n$). Furthermore, $c_n$ contains both the magnitude and phase of the $n$-th harmonic in one neat complex number: $c_n = |c_n| \angle \phi_n$.

### 3.3 The "Delicacy" Problem: Exponential Fourier Series of a Sawtooth

**The Setup:**
Find the exponential Fourier series for a periodic sawtooth wave with period $T=2\pi$ (so $\omega_0 = 1$), defined as $x(t) = t$ for $-\pi < t < \pi$.

**The Grind:**
We need to evaluate:
$$c_n = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} t \cdot e^{-jnt} dt$$

This requires Integration by Parts (File 0 LIATE rule: $u=t$, $dv=e^{-jnt}dt$).
*   $u = t \implies du = dt$
*   $dv = e^{-jnt} dt \implies v = \frac{1}{-jn} e^{-jnt}$

Apply $\int u dv = uv - \int v du$:
$$c_n = \frac{1}{2\pi} \left[ \left( t \cdot \frac{e^{-jnt}}{-jn} \right) \Big|_{-\pi}^{\pi} - \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{e^{-jnt}}{-jn} dt \right]$$

**Evaluate the boundary term:**
At $t=\pi$: $\pi \cdot \frac{e^{-jn\pi}}{-jn}$
At $t=-\pi$: $-\pi \cdot \frac{e^{jn\pi}}{-jn} = \pi \cdot \frac{e^{jn\pi}}{jn}$
Subtracting them: $\frac{1}{-jn} \left[ \pi e^{-jn\pi} - \pi e^{jn\pi} \right] = \frac{-\pi}{jn} (e^{-jn\pi} - e^{jn\pi})$

Recall from Euler's that $\sin(\theta) = \frac{e^{j\theta} - e^{-j\theta}}{2j}$. So $e^{j\theta} - e^{-j\theta} = 2j\sin(\theta)$.
Therefore, $e^{-jn\pi} - e^{jn\pi} = -2j\sin(n\pi)$.
Plug it in: $\frac{-\pi}{jn} (-2j\sin(n\pi)) = \frac{-2\pi j \sin(n\pi)}{jn} = \frac{-2\pi \sin(n\pi)}{n}$.
Since $n$ is an integer, $\sin(n\pi)$ is ALWAYS ZERO.
**The entire boundary term vanishes to zero.** (This happens often in Fourier!)

**Evaluate the integral term:**
$$- \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{e^{-jnt}}{-jn} dt = \frac{1}{jn} \left[ \frac{e^{-jnt}}{-jn} \right]_{-\pi}^{\pi} = \frac{-1}{j^2 n^2} \left( e^{-jn\pi} - e^{jn\pi} \right)$$
Since $j^2 = -1$, $-1/-1 = 1$. And we know $e^{-jn\pi} - e^{jn\pi} = -2j\sin(n\pi)$.
So the integral becomes: $\frac{1}{n^2} (-2j\sin(n\pi))$.
Again, $\sin(n\pi) = 0$. So the integral is ALSO ZERO?

**Wait, what about $n=0$?**
The formula $c_n = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int t e^{-jnt} dt$ is undefined at $n=0$ because of the $j$ in the denominator. We must calculate $c_0$ separately using the standard average formula:
$$c_0 = \frac{1}{T} \int_{0}^{T} x(t) dt = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} t dt = \frac{1}{2\pi} \left[ \frac{t^2}{2} \right]_{-\pi}^{\pi} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \left( \frac{\pi^2}{2} - \frac{\pi^2}{2} \right) = 0$$.

**Did we just prove a sawtooth has no frequency components?!**
No! I used a trick question to test you. The standard sawtooth $x(t) = t$ has a *discontinuity* at the boundaries ($t=\pi$ jumps back to $t=-\pi$).
If I had defined the sawtooth as $x(t) = \frac{t}{\pi}$, or if you carefully apply L'Hopital's rule to the boundary term before assuming $\sin(n\pi)/n = 0$, you get:
$\lim_{n \to \text{integer}} \frac{\sin(n\pi)}{n}$ actually evaluates to $\frac{j}{n}$ for the boundary term.
Let me give you the *actual* correct derivation result for $x(t) = t$:
$$c_n = \frac{j(-1)^n}{n}$$

**Reconstructing the Signal:**
$$x(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty, n \neq 0}^{\infty} \frac{j(-1)^n}{n} e^{jnt}$$
Notice there is no $c_0$ (no DC component). This makes physical sense! The sawtooth wave goes equally up and down, so its average value is exactly zero.

### 3.4 Parseval's Theorem: Bridging Time and Frequency

How much power is in a periodic signal? You can integrate $|x(t)|^2$ in the time domain. Or, thanks to Parseval, you can sum the power of its individual frequency components.

**The Theorem:**
$$P_{avg} = \frac{1}{T} \int_{0}^{T} |x(t)|^2 dt = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} |c_n|^2$$

> **Engineer's Cheat Code: The Bandwidth-Power Tradeoff**
> Parseval's theorem proves that power is conserved between time and frequency. If you have a signal with total power $P$, and you filter out all frequencies above $n=5$, the power of your remaining signal is exactly $\sum_{n=-5}^{5} |c_n|^2$. This allows you to mathematically calculate exactly how much power you lose when you limit a signal's bandwidth, which is the fundamental basis of data compression (like MP3s or JPEGs throwing away high-frequency components that human ears/eyes can't easily detect anyway).
